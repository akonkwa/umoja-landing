# Executive Summary

Winko proposes a revised solar PV, battery storage, and automated panel-cleaning solution for Edward Francis Small Teaching Hospital (EFSTH) in Banjul, The Gambia, to strengthen power continuity for the oxygen production system and other agreed critical loads. The revised configuration increases solar capacity from 140 kWp to 170 kWp and usable battery storage from 522 kWh to 622 kWh, while retaining the 125 kW hybrid inverter platform validated against the hospital's site load profile.

The system is designed around the load conditions confirmed during the January 19, 2026 site validation: a 15 kW base load, 25 kW average load, 35 kW peak cyclic compressor demand, and approximately 600 kWh/day of energy demand. The added 30 kWp of PV improves daytime production and battery-charging headroom, while the added 100 kWh of storage increases resilience during grid interruptions, especially during late-day and overnight operating periods. The integrated automated cleaning system is particularly important for EFSTH because Harmattan dust, salt residue, and biological soiling can materially reduce real PV output if left unmanaged.

From a commercial perspective, the revised total project cost is USD 583,900, comprising the original EPC system price of USD 411,900, an additional USD 92,000 for the expanded PV and battery scope, and USD 80,000 for the automated cleaning system. Hardware mapping indicates a revised hardware value of about USD 234,612, led by battery storage at USD 101,284 and PV modules at USD 69,214. The balance reflects logistics, installation, engineering, commissioning, and EPC performance responsibility rather than equipment supply alone.

The cost-benefit case is strong at an operational level. The incremental investment directly improves energy availability for oxygen production, reduces outage risk for critical hospital functions, increases solar yield in a harsh coastal environment, and lowers the likelihood of underperformance caused by dust-related losses. In practical terms, EFSTH is not only buying more equipment, but also better resilience, stronger daytime generation margin, longer battery-backed support, and more reliable real-world output over time.

Implementation is structured in three phases: 2 to 3 weeks for planning and validation, 6 to 10 weeks for procurement, shipping, and installation, and 2 to 3 weeks for testing, commissioning, handover, and training. Based on the current draft, the proposed payment schedule aligns to these milestones and spans approximately August 1, 2026 through December 29, 2026.

Overall, the revised proposal positions Winko to deliver a hospital-grade critical-load energy system, not just a hardware package. The recommended scope is justified by the need to protect oxygen-system continuity, improve delivered solar performance, and provide EFSTH with a more robust and maintainable energy solution under real operating conditions.
